## Q2. Chinese dish at Shanga (intersecting chords)

**Concepts:** intersecting chords theorem, chord and perpendicular bisector.

Archaeologists at Shanga on Pate Island on Kenya's coast find a broken fragment of the rim of a circular clay Chinese dish, dating to the 15th century (https://www.seaceramic.org.sg/wp-content/uploads/East_African_Coast-1.pdf, https://www.artzip.org/investigation-chinese-ceramics-discovered-kenya-major-conclusions).

(Can use this image: https://www.iias.asia/the-newsletter/article/longquan-celadon-found-kenya)

To find the dish's radius, a student lays a ruler across the fragment: the chord is 24 cm long, and the greatest distance from the chord to the rim is 4 cm.

(a) Why does the perpendicular from the midpoint of the chord pass through the diameter, and why is this the greatest distance from the chord to the rim?

(b) Use the intersecting chords theorem to find the original radius.


**Answer:** 
(a) By symmetry of the circle [find a better answer]
(b) Rest of diameter is $(2R-4)$. By intersecting circle theorem, $12*12 = 4*(2R-4)$, so $R = 20$.

## Q3. Driving Past the Lake (constructing tangents)

**Concepts:** constructing a tangent from an external point; tangent ⟂ radius; Pythagoras.

A town at point $P$ must be connected by road to a town at $T$, $56$km away, but a circular lake of radius $9$km m lies in the way. The shortest route is tangent to the lake and runs alongside it. The centre $O$ of the lake is $15$km from $T$.

(a) Draw a scale diagram, and using ruler and compasses only, construct the tangent from $P$ to the circle, and from $T$ to the circle.
(b) Calculate the length of the tangents
(c) Calculate the arc length around the circle that the road travels.
(d) How long is the shortest road that avoids the lake?

**Answer:** 
(b) First tangent is sqrt(41^2 - 9^2) = 40, second tangent is sqrt(15^2 - 9^2) = 12 km

# Q4 The Arena at Bomas (alternate segment theorem)
 
**Concepts:** alternate segment theorem; angles in the same segment; angles in opposite segments (cyclic quadrilateral).
 
Spectators sit around the edge of a circular performance arena, like the one at Bomas of Kenya. A straight perimeter road runs *tangent* to the arena at the entrance gate $T$. During a performance, a dance troupe processes in a straight line across the arena, from the entrance gate $T$ to the exit gate $A$ on the far side. At the entrance, the procession path makes an angle of $48°$ with the road.
 
(a) Draw the diagram: the circle, the tangent road at $T$, the chord $TA$, and a spectator at a seat $B$ on the major arc $TA$.
 
(b) The spectator at $B$ watches the dancers cross. Through what angle must she turn her head to look from the entrance gate to the exit gate?
 
(c) Her friend is sitting in a completely different seat, also on the major arc. Show that he turns his head through exactly the same angle, wherever on that arc he sits.
 
(d) A drummer sits on the *minor* arc $TA$, between the two gates. Through what angle does *he* turn his head to look from one gate to the other?
 
(e) **Extension:** the arena has radius 15 m. How far do the dancers walk from gate to gate?
 
**Answers:**
 
(b) $48°$. The angle between the tangent (road) and the chord (procession path) equals the angle the chord subtends in the alternate segment, so $\angle TBA = 48°$.
 
(c) Angles in the same segment are equal: every point on the major arc gives the same inscribed angle $48°$. (Nice discussion point: there is no "best seat" for watching the crossing — every spectator on the major arc gets the same viewing angle.)
 
(d) $180° - 48° = 132°$ (opposite angles of cyclic quadrilateral $TBA D$, or: angles in opposite segments are supplementary). He has to swivel much further — he's close to the action.
 
(e) Angle at centre $= 2 \times 48° = 96°$. Chord $TA = 2R\sin 48° \approx 2 \times 15 \times 0.7431 \approx 22.3$ m. (Or via the isosceles triangle $OTA$: $TA = 2 \times 15 \sin 48°$.)

## Q6. The Posho Mill Belt (direct tangents — the belt problem)

**Concepts:** length of a direct (external) common tangent; arc length.

A diesel posho mill in a market centre is driven by a flat belt connecting the engine pulley (radius 6 cm) to the mill pulley (radius 21 cm). The pulley centres are 60 cm apart and the belt runs as an *open* (uncrossed) loop, following the two direct common tangents.

(a) Show that each straight section of the belt has length $L = \sqrt{d^2 - (R-r)^2}$ and calculate it.
(b) **Harder:** find the total belt length, including the two arcs. *(The tangent makes angle $\varphi = \sin^{-1}\frac{R-r}{d}$ with the line of centres; wrap angles are $\pi + 2\varphi$ and $\pi - 2\varphi$.)*

**Answers:** (a) $L = \sqrt{3375} \approx 58.1$ cm. (b) $\varphi \approx 14.5°$; total $\approx 208.6$ cm. Posho mills with belt-and-pulley drives are sold and repaired across rural Kenya, so students may literally have seen this machine.

## Q7. The Boda Boda Chain (direct tangents + ratios)

**Concepts:** direct common tangent; linked rotation of tangent circles.

On a boda boda motorcycle, the engine sprocket has radius 3 cm and the rear-wheel sprocket has radius 9 cm; their centres are 50 cm apart. The chain follows the direct common tangents.

(a) Find the length of each straight run of chain. **Answer:** $\sqrt{2500 - 36} \approx 49.6$ cm.
(b) The chain moves at the same speed everywhere. If the engine sprocket turns at 1 800 revolutions per minute, find the rear sprocket's speed. **Answer:** $1800 \times \tfrac{3}{9} = 600$ rpm — the gear ratio is the ratio of radii.
(c) Discuss: why does a smaller front sprocket help a boda climb hills with a passenger?

## Q8. The Jua Kali Crossed Belt (transverse tangents)

**Concepts:** length of a transverse (internal) common tangent; why crossed belts reverse rotation.

In a jua kali workshop in Gikomba, an old lathe is driven by a *crossed* belt so that the two shafts rotate in opposite directions. The pulleys have radii 10 cm and 15 cm and centres 65 cm apart; the crossed belt follows the transverse common tangents.

(a) Show the straight sections have length $L = \sqrt{d^2 - (R+r)^2}$ and compute it.
(b) Explain with a diagram why crossing the belt reverses the direction of rotation, while an open belt (Q6) preserves it.

**Answer:** $L = \sqrt{4225 - 625} = 60$ cm exactly. Nice contrast with Q6: the only change in the formula is $(R-r) \to (R+r)$.

## Q9. Checking the SGR Curve (intersecting chords — the versine method)

**Concepts:** intersecting chords theorem on a chord and diameter.

Railway engineers on the Standard Gauge Railway check curved track using the **versine method**: a string (chord) of length 20 m is stretched between two points on the rail, and the offset from the string's midpoint to the rail (the versine $v$) is measured. On one curve, $v = 5$ cm.

(a) Using intersecting chords ($10 \times 10 = v(2R - v)$), find the radius of the curve.
(b) Since $v$ is tiny compared with $R$, show the handy site formula $R \approx \dfrac{c^2}{8v}$ for chord $c$.

**Answer:** $R \approx 1\,000$ m. This is the actual method used in track maintenance worldwide — the maths the students are learning is used, unchanged, on the railway they may have ridden.

## Q10. The Path Through Karura (tangent–secant / power of a point)

**Concepts:** intersecting secants and tangent–secant theorem ($PT^2 = PA \cdot PB$).

From a junction $P$ outside a circular thicket in Karura Forest, one footpath just grazes the thicket, touching it at $T$ with $PT = 24$ m. A straight nature trail from the same junction enters the thicket at $A$ and leaves at $B$, with $PA = 16$ m.

(a) Find $PB$, and hence the distance $AB$ the trail spends inside the thicket.
(b) A second trail from $P$ enters at $C$ with $PC = 18$ m. Find $PD$ without any new measurements. *(Power of the point $P$ is the same for every line: $PA \cdot PB = PC \cdot PD = PT^2$.)*

**Answers:** (a) $PB = 576/16 = 36$ m, so $AB = 20$ m. (b) $PD = 576/18 = 32$ m.

## Q11. The Perfect Conversion Kick (tangent–secant, optimisation)

**Concepts:** power of a point; tangent as the limiting/optimal case; circle through two points tangent to a line.

Kenya Sevens score a try. The conversion must be kicked from a line perpendicular to the try line through the point where the try was scored. The posts are 5.6 m apart; the try was scored 10 m outside the nearer post, so the kicker's line passes 10 m from one post and 15.6 m from the other (measured along the try line).

The angle subtended by the posts at the kicker is largest where the circle through both posts is *tangent* to the kicker's line (any circle that *cuts* the line corresponds to a bigger circle and smaller angle — angles in the same segment).

(a) Explain why tangency gives the maximum angle.
(b) Use the tangent–secant relationship to show the best distance from the try line is $x = \sqrt{10 \times 15.6}$ and evaluate it.

**Answer:** $x \approx 12.5$ m. A genuinely famous applied-geometry problem, and Kenya's rugby sevens culture makes it land well.

## Q12. The Round Table from the Triangular Offcut (inscribed circle)

**Concepts:** incircle; $r = \dfrac{\text{Area}}{s}$ (area over semi-perimeter); incentre as intersection of angle bisectors.

A fundi in Kibuye market, Kisumu, has a triangular offcut of mvule timber with sides 1.3 m, 1.4 m and 1.5 m. He wants to cut the largest possible circular table top from it.

(a) Explain why the largest circle is the incircle, and how he could locate its centre with a string-and-chalk angle-bisector construction.
(b) Find the area of the triangle (Heron's formula) and hence the radius of the table top.

**Answer:** $s = 2.1$ m, Area $= 0.84$ m², $r = 0.4$ m — an 80 cm diameter table.

## Q13. One Mast for Three Markets (circumscribed circle)

**Concepts:** circumcircle; circumcentre as intersection of perpendicular bisectors; $R = \dfrac{abc}{4 \times \text{Area}}$.

A network company wants to place one signal mast exactly the same distance from three market centres, which form a triangle with sides 13 km, 14 km and 15 km.

(a) Explain why the mast must sit at the circumcentre, and describe the perpendicular-bisector construction on a scale drawing.
(b) Calculate the common distance (the circumradius).
(c) Discuss: for what kind of triangle would the circumcentre fall *outside* the triangle, and would that placement still make sense?

**Answer:** Area $= 84$ km², $R = \dfrac{13 \times 14 \times 15}{4 \times 84} = 8.125$ km. (Same 13–14–15 triangle as Q12 scaled up — deliberate, so Q15 can tie them together.)

## Q14. The Roundabout in the Road Fork (escribed circle)

**Concepts:** excircle; $r_A = \dfrac{\text{Area}}{s - a}$; excentre as intersection of one internal and two external bisectors.

A triangular traffic island sits where two roads fork. County engineers want a circular flower bed *outside* the island, tangent to the island's rear side and to the two roads (the extensions of the other two sides) — exactly an escribed circle of the triangle.

For the island with sides $a = 13$, $b = 14$, $c = 15$ (in metres), find the radii of all three possible escribed circles, and identify which corresponds to the bed tangent to the 13 m rear side.

**Answers:** $r_a = \dfrac{84}{s-13} = 10.5$ m, $r_b = 12$ m, $r_c = 14$ m. The bed at the rear side has radius 10.5 m.

## Q15. Euler's Hidden Bridge (fun result: incircle meets circumcircle)

**Concepts:** synthesis of inscribed and circumscribed circles; inequality reasoning.

Euler proved that in *any* triangle, the distance $d$ between the incentre and circumcentre satisfies
$$d^2 = R(R - 2r).$$

(a) For the 13–14–15 triangle of Q12/Q13 ($r = 4$, $R = 8.125$ in suitable units), find $d$. **Answer:** $d = \sqrt{8.125 \times 0.125} \approx 1.01$ — the two centres are surprisingly close.
(b) Since $d^2 \ge 0$, deduce **Euler's inequality** $R \ge 2r$ for every triangle. When is $R = 2r$? *(Equilateral.)*
(c) Have students verify $d$ by accurate construction of both centres on the same triangle.

## Q16. Three Sodas and a Bottle Top (fun result: Descartes' circle theorem)

**Concepts:** tangent circles; curvature $k = 1/r$.

Three soda bottles, each of radius 4 cm, stand touching each other in a crate. What is the radius of the largest circular bottle top that can stand flat on the crate floor in the gap between them?

Introduce **Descartes' circle theorem** (1643): for four mutually tangent circles, $k_4 = k_1 + k_2 + k_3 + 2\sqrt{k_1 k_2 + k_2 k_3 + k_3 k_1}$.

With $k_1 = k_2 = k_3 = \tfrac14$: $k_4 = \tfrac34 + 2\sqrt{\tfrac{3}{16}} \approx 1.616$, so $r_4 \approx 0.62$ cm.

(Accessible alternative for (a): find the distance from the centroid of the three bottle centres to one centre, subtract 4 — gets the same answer with just trigonometry, then Descartes confirms it.)

## Q17. The Pappus Chain (fun result / capstone)

**Concepts:** chains of tangent circles; conjecture from accurate construction. Pairs naturally with the existing "circular tree" exercise in this section.

Draw a large semicircle of radius 12 cm on diameter $AC$, and inside it a semicircle of radius 6 cm on $AB$ (where $B$ is the midpoint of $AC$). The crescent region between them is an *arbelos*. Now inscribe a chain of circles in the crescent, each tangent to both semicircles and to the previous circle — the **Pappus chain**, studied by Pappus of Alexandria in the 4th century.

(a) Construct (or measure on a printed figure) the first two or three circles of the chain.
(b) For each circle, measure the height of its centre above $AC$ and divide by its diameter. Conjecture the pattern.
(c) State Pappus' theorem: the centre of the $n$th circle is exactly $n$ diameters above the line. *(With this configuration, circle 1 has radius 2 cm at height 4 cm; circle 2 has radius 1 cm at height 4 cm... verify: $r_n = \dfrac{12}{2n^2+4}$, height $= 2n r_n$.)*
(d) Discussion: Pappus proved this with pages of Euclidean geometry; today one line of "circle inversion" does it. Sometimes mathematics waits 1 500 years for the right tool.

## [REJECTED] Q1. The Rolling Shilling (warm-up / fun result)

**Concepts:** tangent circles, point of tangency, circumference.

Two identical Ksh 20 coins lie flat on a desk. One is rolled (without slipping) all the way around the rim of the other. **How many full rotations does it make?**

Most students answer 1 (equal circumferences). The answer is **2** — the coin rotation paradox. The centre of the rolling coin travels a circle of radius $2r$, so it turns through $2\pi \cdot 2r / (2\pi r) = 2$ rotations. Great as a hands-on opener with real coins; the point of contact is always the point of tangency of two externally tangent circles.
