    Teacher Resource 2.5.1. Problem-Solving and Discovery.

    Suggested Time
    🔗
    15 minutes
    🔗
    Objective
    🔗
    To realize that fractions are easier to compare and compute when the denominator is a rational number.
    🔗
    Teacher’s Role
    🔗
    Write \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\) on the board. Ask learners to estimate its value using a calculator. Then ask them to multiply both the numerator and denominator by \(\sqrt{2}\) and estimate again. Lead a discussion on why \(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\) might be more “useful” than \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Learner Experience 2.5.1.

    Investigate the effect of multiplying a fraction by a specialized form of 1:
    🔗
    1. Consider the fraction \(\frac{3}{\sqrt{5}}\text{.}\) If you multiply it by \(\frac{\sqrt{5}}{\sqrt{5}}\text{,}\) what happens to the radical in the denominator?
      🔗
    2. Does the actual value of the number change when you multiply it by \(\frac{\sqrt{n}}{\sqrt{n}}\text{?}\) Explain why.
      🔗
    3. Try to find a way to remove the radical from the denominator of \(\frac{1}{2 + \sqrt{3}}\text{.}\) (Hint: Remember the difference of two squares from your previous algebra lessons).
      🔗
    🔗

    Teacher Resource 2.5.2. Structured Instruction.

    Suggested Time
    🔗
    10 minutes
    🔗
    Objective
    🔗
    To formalize the process of rationalising denominators for both monomial and binomial surds.
    🔗
    🔗

    Key Takeaways & Teacher Connection.

    1. Connecting to Learner Work: Explain that rationalising is like “cleaning up” a fraction. For binomial denominators, introduce the concept of the conjugate.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Key Takeaway 2.5.3.

    Rationalising the denominator is the process of removing a radical from the bottom of a fraction.
    🔗
    • For a single surd \(\sqrt{a}\text{,}\) multiply the top and bottom by \(\sqrt{a}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • For a binomial \(a + \sqrt{b}\text{,}\) multiply by its conjugate \(a - \sqrt{b}\text{.}\) The product of a surd and its conjugate is always a rational number.
      🔗
    🔗

    Teacher Resource 2.5.4. Practice and Application.

    Suggested Time
    🔗
    10 minutes
    🔗
    Objective
    🔗
    To apply the learned concepts and procedures to solve varied problems.
    🔗
    Teacher’s Role
    🔗
    The teacher works through the following examples with the class, asking questions and connecting to insights from the learner experience.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.5.

    Rationalise the denominator of \(\frac{5}{\sqrt{3}}\)

    Solution.

    Multiply the numerator and denominator by \(\sqrt{3}\text{:}\) \(\frac{5 \times \sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{3}} = \frac{5\sqrt{3}}{3}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.6.

    Simplify by rationalising: \(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{6}}\)

    Solution.

    First, simplify the fraction: \(\sqrt{\frac{2}{6}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{3}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\text{.}\) Now rationalise: \(\frac{1 \times \sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{3}} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.7.

    Rationalise the denominator of \(\frac{2}{3 - \sqrt{5}}\)

    Solution.

    Multiply by the conjugate \(3 + \sqrt{5}\text{:}\) \(\frac{2(3 + \sqrt{5})}{(3 - \sqrt{5})(3 + \sqrt{5})}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Denominator: \(3^2 - (\sqrt{5})^2 = 9 - 5 = 4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Result: \(\frac{2(3 + \sqrt{5})}{4} = \frac{3 + \sqrt{5}}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.8.

    Rationalise: \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} + 1}\)

    Solution.

    Multiply by the conjugate \(\sqrt{2} - 1\text{:}\) \(\frac{1(\sqrt{2} - 1)}{(\sqrt{2} + 1)(\sqrt{2} - 1)} = \frac{\sqrt{2} - 1}{2 - 1} = \sqrt{2} - 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.9.

    Simplify \(\frac{\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{3} - \sqrt{2}}\) by rationalising the denominator.

    Solution.

    Multiply by the conjugate \(\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{2}\text{:}\) \(\frac{(\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{2})^2}{(\sqrt{3} - \sqrt{2})(\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{2})}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Numerator: \(3 + 2\sqrt{6} + 2 = 5 + 2\sqrt{6}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Denominator: \(3 - 2 = 1\text{.}\) Result: \(5 + 2\sqrt{6}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Teacher Resource 2.5.10. Assessment / Evaluate.

    Suggested Time
    🔗
    8 minutes
    🔗
    Objective
    🔗
    To formatively assess individual Learner understanding of the lesson objectives.
    🔗
    Learning Experience
    🔗
    Learners independently complete a short assessment task (select from the exercises below).
    🔗
    Teacher’s Role
    🔗
    Collect and review the exit tickets to gauge Learner understanding and identify any common misconceptions that need to be addressed in the next lesson.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Exercises

    1.

    Rationalise the denominator of \(\frac{7}{\sqrt{11}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(\frac{7\sqrt{11}}{11}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Simplify \(\frac{4}{2\sqrt{2}}\) by rationalising.
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(\frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{2} = \sqrt{2}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Rationalise the denominator of \(\frac{1}{4 + \sqrt{7}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(\frac{4 - \sqrt{7}}{16 - 7} = \frac{4 - \sqrt{7}}{9}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    A triangle has an area of 10 square units and a base of \(\sqrt{5}\) units. Find its height and write the answer with a rationalised denominator.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Area = \(\frac{1}{2}bh \implies 10 = \frac{1}{2}(\sqrt{5})h \implies h = \frac{20}{\sqrt{5}} = \frac{20\sqrt{5}}{5} = 4\sqrt{5}\) units.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    In a technical drawing, a length is given by \(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2 + \sqrt{3}}\text{.}\) Express this length in a form without a radical in the denominator.
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(\frac{\sqrt{3}(2 - \sqrt{3})}{(2 + \sqrt{3})(2 - \sqrt{3})} = \frac{2\sqrt{3} - 3}{4 - 3} = 2\sqrt{3} - 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Simplify \(\frac{2}{\sqrt{10} - \sqrt{8}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(\frac{2(\sqrt{10} + \sqrt{8})}{10 - 8} = \frac{2(\sqrt{10} + 2\sqrt{2})}{2} = \sqrt{10} + 2\sqrt{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    7.

    Two numbers are in the ratio \(1 : (2 + \sqrt{3})\text{.}\) Express this ratio in its simplest rationalised form \((a - \sqrt{b}) : 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Divide 1 by \(2 + \sqrt{3}\) and rationalise: \(\frac{1(2 - \sqrt{3})}{(2 + \sqrt{3})(2 - \sqrt{3})} = 2 - \sqrt{3}\text{.}\) Ratio is \((2 - \sqrt{3}) : 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
