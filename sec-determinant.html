    Teacher Resource 7.7.1. Problem-Solving and Discovery.

    Suggested Time
    🔗
    10 minutes
    🔗
    Objective
    🔗
    To provide a visual explanation for the formula for the determinant.
    🔗
    Teacher’s Role
    🔗
    If time allows, prepare the shapes from Figure 7.7.4 in advance, so that you can cut them out and rearrange them with the class during the activity. If not, you can ask learners to draw and cut out the shapes themselves.
    🔗
    🔗

    Learner Experience 7.7.1.

    Visualising a Matrix.

    Suppose \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \amp 2.5 \\ 2.2 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix}\)
    1. If \(\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 0\end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) find \(A\mathbf{x}\) and \(A\mathbf{y}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      Solution.
      \(A\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}6 \\ 2.2\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) the first column of \(A\) and \(A\mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix}2.5 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) the second column of \(A\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Plot the vectors \(A\mathbf{x}\) and \(A\mathbf{y}\) on the coordinate plane, with their tails at the origin
      🔗
      Solution.
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Also plot a point representing the sum \(A\mathbf{x} + A\mathbf{y}\text{,}\) and connect the tips of your previous vectors to this point to form a parallelogram.
      🔗
      Solution.
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    The determinant of a matrix tells us how it changes areas (like the parallelogram we just created). We’ll explore how to find it.
    🔗
    🔗

    Finding an Expression for the Determinant.

    We’ll consider how the general matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix}a \amp b\\ c \amp d\end{bmatrix}\) changes areas. In general, we have:
    \begin{align*} A\mathbf{x} \amp = \begin{bmatrix}a \amp b\\ c \amp d\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}a \\ c\end{bmatrix} \\ A\mathbf{y} \amp = \begin{bmatrix}a \amp b\\ c \amp d\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}b \\ d\end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    To do so, this figure will help:
    1. Copy out Figure 7.7.4 into a loose piece of paper, label all the side lengths (on the inside!).
      🔗
      🔗
    2. What’s the total area of the large rectangle?
      🔗
      Solution.
      The area of the large rectangle is given by the product of its width and height, which are \(a + b\) and \(c + d\) respectively. Therefore, the area of the large rectangle is:
      \begin{align} \text{Total Area} \amp = (a + b)(c + d) \notag\\ \amp = ac + ad + bc + bd \tag{7.7.1} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Cut out all the coloured triangles and squares. Can you find a helpful way to rearrange them to figure out the total coloured area?
      🔗
      Solution.
      The two red triangles can be combined to form a rectangle with area \(ac\text{,}\) and the two blue triangles can be combined to form a rectangle with area \(bd\text{.}\) Thus the combined area is:
      \begin{align} \text{Coloured Area} \amp = \color{red}{\text{red area}} + \color{blue}{\text{blue area}} + \color{orange}{\text{orange area}} \notag\\ \amp = \color{red}{ac} + \color{blue}{bd} + \color{orange}{2bc} \tag{7.7.2} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗

      Note 7.7.5.

      Gently guide Learners if they are struggling to find the area. There are many different ways to rearrange the shapes, so encourage Learners to be creative.
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    4. What is the area of the parallelogram \(D\text{?}\)
      🔗
      Solution.
      The area of the parallelogram \(D\) is given by the total area of the large rectangle minus the coloured area. Thus, using (7.7.1) and (7.7.2), we have:
      \begin{align*} \text{Area of } D \amp = \text{Total Area} - \text{Coloured Area} \\ \amp = (ac + ad + bc + bd) - (ac + bd + 2bc) \\ \amp = ad + bc - 2bc \\ \amp = ad - bc \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Teacher Resource 7.7.6. Structured Instruction.

    Suggested Time
    🔗
    5 minutes
    🔗
    Objective
    🔗
    To formalize the concepts and properties learners discovered in the anchor task.
    🔗
    🔗

    Key Takeaways & Teacher Connection.

    • Make sure Learners recognise the area \(D = ad - bc\) from the Learner Experience is the determinant of the matrix \(A\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Stress the connection between determinants and inverses. Relate this to how we cannot divide by zero.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Remind Learners that they only need to find the determinant of 2 by 2 matrices in this course. Point them to Technology 7.7.9 if they are curious about higher dimensions.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 7.7.7. The Determinant.

    If \(A = \begin{bmatrix}a \amp b \\ c \amp d\end{bmatrix}\) is a 2 by 2 matrix, then the determinant of \(A\) is given by the formula
    \begin{equation*} \det{A} = ad - bc\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Key Takeaway 7.7.8.

    • The determinant measures how an area changes when we apply a matrix to it.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The determinant of a matrix \(A\) is denoted by \(\det{A}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(A = \begin{bmatrix}a \amp b \\ c \amp d\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) then \(\det{A} = ad - bc\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    The Determinant and the Inverse.

    • You might recognise this expression from the formula for the inverse of a 2 by 2 matrix. We have:
      \begin{equation*} A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix}a \amp b \\ c \amp d\end{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\det{A}}\begin{bmatrix}d \amp -b \\ -c \amp a\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • If the determinant of a matrix is zero, then the matrix does not have an inverse.
      🔗
      🔗
    • This is because the matrix “collapses” areas to zero, meaning that we cannot reverse the transformation to get back to our original vectors.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.7.11.

    If \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) compute the determinant of \(A\text{,}\) written \(\det{A}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Solution.

    We can compute the determinant of a 2 by 2 matrix using the formula \(\det{\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}} = ad - bc\text{.}\) In this case, we have \(a = 5\text{,}\) \(b = 3\text{,}\) \(c = 1\text{,}\) and \(d = 4\text{.}\) Substituting these values into the formula gives us:
    \begin{align*} \det{A} \amp = (5)(4) - (3)(1) \\ \amp = 20 - 3 \\ \amp = 17 \end{align*}
    Therefore, the determinant of matrix \(A\) is \(17\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.7.12.

    Let \(M = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 4 & -2 \end{bmatrix}\)
    1. Compute \(\det{M}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. What does the value of the determinant tell us about the inverse of \(M\text{?}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Solution.

    1. Using the formula for the determinant of a 2 by 2 matrix, we have:
      \begin{align*} \det{M} \amp = ad - bc \\ \amp = (2)(-2) - (-1)(4) \\ \amp = -4 + 4 \\ \amp = 0 \end{align*}
      Therefore, the determinant of matrix \(M\) is \(0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Since \(\det{M} = 0\text{,}\) the matrix \(M\) does not have an inverse.
      🔗
      This is because any system of equations \(M\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) would have either no solutions or infinitely many solutions, depending on the value of \(\mathbf{b}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      For example, if \(\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) then we get the system of equations:
      \begin{align*} 2x - y \amp = 0 \\ 4x - 2y \amp = 0 \end{align*}
      These equations represent the same line, so the system has infinitely many solutions.
      🔗
      In either case, we cannot find a unique solution for \(\mathbf{x}\text{,}\) which is why the matrix \(M\) does not have an inverse.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.7.13.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix}-4 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 3\end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    1. Compute \(\det{A}\) and \(\det{B}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Find the product \(AB\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Compute \(\det{AB}\) and verify that it is equal to \(\det{A} \cdot \det{B}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Solution.

    1. Finding \(\det{A}\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \det{A} \amp = ad - bc \\ \amp = (-4)(3) - (0)(0) \\ \amp = -12 \end{align*}
      Finding \(\det{B}\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \det{B} \amp = ad - bc \\ \amp = (1)(2) - (2)(3) \\ \amp = 2 - 6 \\ \amp = -4 \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. To find the product \(AB\text{,}\) we perform matrix multiplication:
      \begin{align*} AB \amp = \begin{bmatrix} -4 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} (-4)(1) + (0)(3) \amp (-4)(2) + (0)(2) \\ (0)(1) + (3)(3) \amp (0)(2) + (3)(2) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} -4 \amp -8 \\ 9 \amp 6 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Finding \(\det{AB}\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \det{AB} \amp = ad - bc \\ \amp = (-4)(6) - (-8)(9) \\ \amp = -24 + 72 \\ \amp = 48 \end{align*}
      Now we verify that \(\det{AB} = \det{A} \cdot \det{B}\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \det{A} \cdot \det{B} \amp = (-12)(-4) \\ \amp = 48 \\ \amp = \det{AB} \end{align*}
      🔗
      In fact, the property \(\det{AB} = \det{A} \cdot \det{B}\) holds for all matrices \(A\) and \(B\) (as long as \(AB\) is defined).
      🔗
      🔗

      🔗
    6. \(\displaystyle \det{AB} = \det{BA} \)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. This statement is false. A counterexample is:
      \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix},\quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
      We have:
      \begin{align*} \det{A} + \det{B} \amp = (1)(1) + (2)(2) \\ \amp = 1 + 4 \\ \amp = 5 \end{align*}
      However, we also have:
      \begin{align*} A + B \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \\ \det{\left(A + B\right)} \amp = (3)(3) - (0)(0) \\ \amp = 9 \end{align*}
      Therefore, \(\det{\left(A + B\right)} = 9\) and \(\det{A} + \det{B} = 5\text{,}\) which shows that the statement is false.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. This statement is false.

      Aside: A Related True Statement.

      In fact, the related true result for any \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\) and scalar \(k\) is:
      \begin{align*} \det{kA} \amp = k^n \det{A} \end{align*}
      🔗
      A counterexample is \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) We have
      \begin{align*} \det{A} \amp = (1)(1) - (0)(0) \\ \amp = 1 \end{align*}
      However, we also have:
      \begin{align*} 2A \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \\ \det{2A} \amp = (2)(2) - (0)(0) \\ \amp = 4 \end{align*}
      Therefore, \(\det{2A} = 4\) and \(2\det{A} = 2\text{,}\) which shows that the statement is false.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. This statement is true. The proof is as follows: Let \(A\) and \(B\) be 2 by 2 matrices. We can write \(A\) and \(B\) as:
      \begin{align*} A \amp = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \\ B \amp = \begin{bmatrix} e & f \\ g & h \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
      Then the product \(AB\) is given by:
      \begin{align*} AB \amp = \begin{bmatrix} ae + bg & af + bh \\ ce + dg & cf + dh \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
      Now we compute the determinant of \(AB\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \det{AB} =\, \amp (ae + bg)(cf + dh) - (af + bh)(ce + dg) \\ =\, \amp aecf + aedh + bgcf + bgdh \\ \amp - afce - afdg - bhce - bhdg \\ = \amp \phantom{+} \cancel{aecf} + aedh + bgcf + \cancel{bgdh} \\ \amp - \cancel{afce} - afdg - bhce - \cancel{bhdg} \\ = \,\amp \phantom{+}adeh - adfg - bceh + bcfg \end{align*}
      Tackling \(\det{A} \cdot \det{B}\text{,}\) we have:
      \begin{align*} \det{A} \cdot \det{B} \amp = (ad - bc)(eh - fg) \\ \amp = adeh - adfg - bceh + bcfg \\ \amp = \det{AB} \end{align*}
      Therefore, we have shown that \(\det{AB} = \det{A} \cdot \det{B}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. This statement is true. The 2 by 2 identity matrix is given by:
      \begin{align*} I \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
      Now we compute the determinant of the identity matrix:
      \begin{align*} \det{I} \amp = (1)(1) - (0)(0) \\ \amp = 1 \end{align*}
      Therefore, we have shown that \(\det{I} = 1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. This statement is false.

      Aside: Another Related True Statement.

      In fact, the related true result for any square matrix \(A\) and power \(k\) is:
      \begin{align*} \det{\left(A^k\right)} \amp = \left(\det{A}\right)^k \end{align*}
      Can you use Item 7.7.3.c to prove this statement?
      🔗
      A counterexample is: \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \) We have:
      \begin{align*} \det{A} \amp = (1)(1) - (0)(0) \\ \amp = 1 \end{align*}
      However,
      \begin{align*} A^2 \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \\ \det{A^2} \amp = (1)(1) - (0)(0) \\ \amp = 1 \end{align*}
      So \(2\det{A} = 2 \neq 1 = \det{\left(A^2\right)}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    6. This statement is true. From (c), we know that
      \begin{equation*} \det{AB} = \det{A} \cdot \det{B} \end{equation*}
      Swapping the roles of \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) we get
      \begin{equation*} \det{BA} = \det{B} \cdot \det{A}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since multiplication of real numbers can be done in any order, we have
      \begin{gather*} \det{AB} = \det{A} \cdot \det{B} = \det{B} \cdot \det{A} = \det{BA} \end{gather*}
      🔗
      This should be surprising: while \(AB \neq BA\) in general, we have \(\det{AB} = \det{BA}\text{.}\)
      🔗

    4. The Determinant of the Inverse.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    1. Compute \(\det{A}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Find \(A^{-1}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Find \(\det{\left(A^{-1}\right)}\text{,}\) the determinant of the inverse of \(A\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. What’s the relationship between \(\det{\left(A^{-1}\right)}\) and \(\det{A}\text{?}\) Can you prove it?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Using the formula:
      \begin{align*} \det{A} \amp = ad - bc \\ \amp = (2)(4) - (1)(3) \\ \amp = 8 - 3 \\ \amp = 5 \end{align*}
      Therefore, \(\det{A} = 5\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. To find the inverse of a 2 by 2 matrix, we can use the formula:
      \begin{align*} A^{-1} \amp = \frac{1}{ad - bc} \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
      Substituting the values from matrix \(A\text{,}\) we get:
      \begin{align*} A^{-1} \amp = \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -1 \\ -3 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{4}{5} & -\frac{1}{5} \\ -\frac{3}{5} & \frac{2}{5} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
      Therefore, the inverse of matrix \(A\) is \(A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{4}{5} & -\frac{1}{5} \\ -\frac{3}{5} & \frac{2}{5} \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Now we compute the determinant of the inverse of \(A\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \det{\left(A^{-1}\right)} \amp = ad - bc \\ \amp = \left(\frac{4}{5}\right)\left(\frac{2}{5}\right) - \left(-\frac{1}{5}\right)\left(-\frac{3}{5}\right) \\ \amp = \frac{8}{25} - \frac{3}{25} \\ \amp = \frac{5}{25} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{5} \end{align*}
      Therefore, \(\det{\left(A^{-1}\right)} = \frac{1}{5}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The relationship between \(\det{\left(A^{-1}\right)}\) and \(\det{A}\) is that \(\det{\left(A^{-1}\right)} = \frac{1}{\det{A}}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      You can prove this using the fact from Item 7.7.3.c that
      \begin{equation*} \det{AB} = \det{A} \cdot \det{B}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Letting \(B = A^{-1}\text{,}\) and remembering that \(AA^{-1} = I\) by definition, we have
      \begin{equation*} (\det{A})(\det{\left(A^{-1}\right)}) = \det{AA^{-1}} = \det{I} = 1\text{,} \end{equation*}
      where \(I\) is the identity matrix.
